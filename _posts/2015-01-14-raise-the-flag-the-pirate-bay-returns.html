Originally written for the Students For Liberty Blogging Series, posted here on 1.13.15.

Let me be the first to lament the ‘fall’ of The Pirate Bay, a service that, statistically speaking, there’s a decent chance you use. On December 9th, Swedish officials decided to raid the facilities that contained the servers that TPB was hosted on, effectively shutting the site down. While TPB has remained ‘offline,’ it’s search engine and catalog no longer available to those who access the site, it’s probably a good time to realize that this is not the end. Nor has it ever been. Or will be.

[caption id="attachment_733" align="aligncenter" width="750"] The Pirate Bay Logo, Source: Unknown[/caption]

This shutdown is but one of countless times in The Pirate Bay’s long, hilarious, and global travels. In 2013 alone, The Pirate Bay had changed domains over six times, 3 of those times happening within a single week. From the Americas to Europe, TPB has set it sails in many waters, sailing to Peru, Iceland, Sweden, Guyana, Sint Maarten, and beyond. During its last episode of chase by international authorities, the owners of TPB had even bragged that they had 70 backup domains set for just this kind of occasion.

Even now, despite all nay sayers, you can visit thepiratebay.se and you’ll give witness to an ominous waving jolly roger flag, with an even more ominous countdown clock set to February 1st. While there are no official statements out yet, one can only assume what is in store for the world on that date. Based on TPB’s record, it’s more than reasonable to assume that we’ll be able to return to our beloved torrenting website that we’re all familiar with.

[caption id="attachment_734" align="aligncenter" width="750"] screen shot on 1.14.15 of thepiratebay.se homepage, Source: lackingmaterial.com[/caption]

What if you want to torrent sooner rather than later? Don’t worry, the internet has you covered with over 300 clones of the original Pirate Bay. Users are being encouraged to create their own copy through ‘Open Bay’, a project designed to be accessible to almost anyone, regardless of technical knowledge. Given the immensely open, easily duplicated, and spreadable nature of peer to peer networking, websites such as ISOHunt and individuals alike have made exact copies of TPB that can be used to find the magnet links necessary to torrent.

Now that we can all relax, what should next be noted is the continued failure of the entertainment industry over Intellectual Property Rights. Despite the countless millions spent on lawyers, law agencies, and services (such as hiring private companies to track users), we see that torrenting remains an ever popular activity that hasn’t stopped. All that this spending has managed to achieve is violating the rights of citizens all over the world. From overly aggressive and unsubstantiated threats from lawyers and copyright laws that act as a censure on the internet, IPR advocates have managed to make a much less free and open world.

Furthermore, attacking entities like The Pirate Bay doesn’t actually help alleviate the rate of piracy. While the number of users momentarily decreased following the December 9th takedown of TPB, they quickly rose to levels identical to those prior. What groups like the Movie Picture Association of America or the British Phonographic Industry are attempting to do is preserve their market shares in an ever changing and innovating world. Attacks against TPB and individual torrenters are symptoms of an industry that wants to maintain its current profits without having to make the necessary investments and innovation, a sentiment that blatantly contrasts against that of the 21st century.

[caption id="attachment_735" align="aligncenter" width="555"] Percent of adults who stream music by country, Source: Universal Music, url: WSJ[/caption]

Currently, there are multiple alternatives forming that counter the current state of affairs. Services like Spotify and Neftlix standout the most and, ironically, have been connected with a substantial decrease in piracy. Why? In exchange for a modest price, users are given wide access to music/movies they normally wouldn’t be able to afford, while IPR(regardless of your opinion on it) is preserved for those who have it. More threatening, sites like Band Camp completely bypass the entertainment industry and instead connect musicians directly to their fans, where prices are much lower and flexible. What we see is that there are real alternatives to the status quo, and the only reason why “things aren’t better” for the entertainment industry is because they’re too blinded by greed to pursue them, favoring abusive and crony practices instead.

[caption id="attachment_737" align="aligncenter" width="750"] Graph plotting the rate of streaming and pirating via P2P networking in Australia, Source/URL: thebestVPN.com[/caption]

The Pirate Bay represents everything that the anachronistic entertainment industry isn’t, and it’s for that reason that we should celebrate both its rise and death. Like squeezing sand in your hand, the attacks against TPB and torrenting only result in more innovation and revolution. When TPB was previously taken down, the owners were the ones who had to put it back up (and always successfully did). Now we see that even in its current death, TPB has managed to be resurrected without the original site having to do a single thing. This innovation and progress isn’t free, and the liberatory aspects of technology must continue to be fought for politically. Bills like The Trans-Pacific Partnership and agencies like the NSA continue to threaten the very nature of the internet. The reality is that no amount of processing power can protect you from oppressive governments, an argument core members of TPB crew agree with. The fight for internet and technological freedom has to be as dynamic as the The Pirate Bay is durable, characteristics that are arguably interlinked. When one goes, the other inevitably will disappear as well.

Let this be turning point for us, I dare say a rallying cry. While The Pirate Bay may be down for now, it sure as hell is not out.
